Wheeled Mobile Robots

Car-like mobile robots

  • A commonly used model for the low-speed behavior of a four-wheeled car-like vehicle is the kinematic bicycle model.
  • The velocity of each wheel is in the plane of the wheel and the wheel rools without slipping sideways: B𝐯=(v,0). {^B}\bm{v} = (v, 0).
  • The configuration of the vehicle is represented by the generalized coordinates 𝐪=(x,y,θ)∈𝒞=ℝ2×𝕊1\bm{q} = (x, y, \theta) \in \mathcal{C} = \mathbb{R}^2 \times \mathbb{S}^1.
  • The dashed lines show the direction along which the wheels cannot move; these lines of no motion intersect at the instantenous center of rotation (ICR).
  • The angular velocity is θ̇=vRB, \dot{\theta} = \frac{v}{R_B}, and by simple geometry the turning radius is RB=LtanγR_B = \frac{L}{\tan \gamma}, where LL is the length of the vehicle or wheel base.

Car-like mobile robots

  • For a fixed steering wheel angle, the car moves along a circular arc.
  • The velocity of the robot in the world frame is (vcosθ,vsinθ)(v \cos{\theta}, v \sin{\theta}) so the equations of motion are

ẋ=vcosθ,ẏ=vsinθ,θ̇=vLtanγ. \begin{aligned} \dot{x} &= v \cos{\theta}, \\ \dot{y} &= v \sin{\theta}, \\ \dot{\theta} &= \frac{v}{L} \tan{\gamma}. \end{aligned}

Characteristics of a car-like vehicle

  • When v=0v=0 then θ̇=0\dot{\theta} = 0; i.e., it is not possible to change the vehicle’s orientation when it is stationary.
  • In the world-coordinate frame, the velocity must satisfy an algebraic equality: ẏcosθ−ẋsinθ≡0, \dot{y}\cos{\theta} - \dot{x}\sin{\theta} \equiv 0, called a nonholonomic constraint. This equation cannot be integrated to form a holonomic constraint, i.e., a relationship between xx, yy, and θ\theta.

Differentially-steered vehicle

  • Differential steering steers by independently controlling the speed of the wheels on each side of the vehicle – if the speeds are not equal the vehicle will turn.
  • The vehicle’s velocity is by definition vv in the vehicle’s xx-direction, and zero in the yy-direction since the wheel’s cannot slip sideways: B𝐯=(v,0). {^B}\bm{v} = (v, 0).
  • The configuration of the vehicle is represented by the generalized coordinates 𝐪=(x,y,θ)∈𝒞=ℝ2×𝕊1\bm{q} = (x, y, \theta) \in \mathcal{C} = \mathbb{R}^2 \times \mathbb{S}^1.
  • The vehicle follows a curved path centered on ICR.
  • The left-hand wheels move at a speed of vLv_L along an arc with a radius RLR_L while the right-hand wheels move at a speed of vRv_R along an arc with a radius of RRR_R. The angular velocity is θ̇=vLRL=vRRR=vR−vLW, \dot{\theta} = \frac{v_L}{R_L} = \frac{v_R}{R_R} = \frac{v_R - v_L}{W}, since RR=RL+WR_R = R_L + W, with WW denoting the wheel separation.

Differentially-steered vehicle

  • The equations of motion are therefore ẋ=vcosθ,ẏ=vsinθ,θ̇=vΔW, \begin{aligned} \dot{x} &= v \cos{\theta}, \\ \dot{y} &= v \sin{\theta}, \\ \dot{\theta} &= \frac{v_\Delta}{W}, \end{aligned} where v=12(vR+vL)v = \frac{1}{2}\left(v_R+v_L\right) and vΔ=vR−vLv_\Delta = v_R - v_L are the average and differential velocities, respectively.
  • For a desired speed vv and turn rate θ̇\dot{\theta} we can solve for vRv_R and vLv_L. This kinematic model is often called the unicycle model.
  • Similar to the car-like vehicle, this model has a nonholonomic constraint as well: ẏcosθ−ẋsinθ=0. \dot{y} \cos{\theta} - \dot{x} \sin{\theta} = 0.
  • If we move the vehicle’s reference frame to {B′}\left\{B^\prime\right\} and ignore orientation, we can rewrite the EoM as (ẋẏ)=[cosθ−asinθsinθacosθ](vω). \begin{pmatrix} \dot{x} \\ \dot{y} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \cos{\theta} & -a \sin{\theta} \\ \sin{\theta} & a \cos{\theta} \end{bmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} v \\ \omega \end{pmatrix}. (To be solved for desired ẋ\dot{x} and ẏ\dot{y}.)

Omnidirectional vehicle

  • Omnidirectional wheels remove the nonholonomic constraint, simplifying control algorithms.
  • Such ‘’Swedish’’ wheels comprises a number of rollers set around a circumference of the wheel with their axes at an angle of α\alpha relative to the axle of the wheel.
  • The rollers have a barrel shape so only one point on the roller is in contact with the ground at any time.
  • Rotation of the wheel will cause a forward velocity of Rω𝐱WR\omega \bm{x}_W where RR is the wheel radius.
  • Because the roller is free to roll in the direction indicated by the green line, normal to the roller’s axis, there is potentially arbitrary velocity in that direction.
  • Resolve a desired velocity 𝐯\bm{v} into two components, one parallel to 𝐱W\bm{x}_W and one parallel to the rolling direction 𝐯=vW𝐱W⏟driven+vr(cosα𝐱W+sinα𝐲W)⏟rolling=(vW+vrcosα)𝐱W+vrsinα𝐲W, \begin{aligned} \bm{v} &= \underbrace{v_W\bm{x}_W}_{\text{driven}} + \underbrace{v_r\left( \cos{\alpha} \bm{x}_W + \sin{\alpha}\bm{y}_W\right)}_{\text{rolling}} \\ &= \left( v_W + v_r \cos{\alpha}\right)\bm{x}_W + v_r \sin{\alpha} \bm{y}_W, \end{aligned}

Flying Robots

Flying robots or unmanned aerial vehicles

  • UAVs are becoming increasingly common and span a huge range of size and shapes.
  • Flying robots have 66-dof with configuration 𝐪∈𝒞=𝐒𝐄(3)\bm{q} \in \mathcal{C} = \bm{SE}(3).
  • We use a dynamic rather than a kinematic model.
  • The rotors are driven by electric motors, powered by electronic speed controllers.
  • The rotor speed is ϖi\varpi_i and the thrust is an upward vector Ti=bϖi2,i=1,2,3,4, T_i = b \varpi_i^2, \; \; i = 1, 2, 3, 4, in the vehicle’s negative zz-direction.
  • The translational dynamics of the vehicle in world coordinates is given by Newton’s second law m𝐯̇=(00mg)−0𝐑B(00T)−B𝐯, m \dot{\bm{v}} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ m g \end{pmatrix} - {^0}\bm{R}_B \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ T \end{pmatrix} - B \bm{v}, where 𝐯\bm{v} is the velocity of the CoM in the world frame, gg is the gravitational acceleration, mm is the total mass, BB is the aerodynamic friction and T=∑iTiT = \sum_i T_i is the total upward thrust.

Flying robots or unmanned aerial vehicles

  • Pairwise differences in rotor thrusts cause the vehicle to rotate. The rolling torque is generated by the moments τx=d(T4−T2)=db(ϖ42−ϖ22), \tau_x = d(T_4 - T_2) = db(\varpi_4^2 - \varpi_2^2), where dd is the distance from the rotor axis to the center of mass.
  • Similarly, the pitching torque is τy=db(ϖ12−ϖ32). \tau_y = db (\varpi_1^2 - \varpi_3^2).
  • The torque applied to each propeller by the motor is opposed by aerodynamic drag Qi=kϖi2. Q_i = k \varpi_i^2.
  • This torque exerts a reaction torque on the airframe which acts to rotate the airframe about the propeller shaft in the opposite direction of rotation τz=Q1−Q2+Q3−Q4=k(ϖ12+ϖ32−ϖ22−ϖ42). \tau_z = Q_1 - Q_2 + Q_3 - Q_4 = k \left( \varpi_1^2 + \varpi_3^2 - \varpi_2^2 - \varpi_4^2 \right).

That is, a yaw torque can be created simply by appropriate coordinated control of all four rotor speeds.
