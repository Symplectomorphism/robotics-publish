Forward Kinematics

Kinematic chains

 

  • A robot manipulator is composed of a set of links connected together by joints.
    • Simple: revolute or prismatic with one degree-of-freedom (DoF)
    • Complex: universal or spherical with multiple DoF
  • No loss of generality considering only single DoF joints as multiple DoF joints can always be thought of as a succession of single DoF joints with links of length zero in between.

 

  • The action of each joint can be described by a single real number:

    • the angle of rotation in the case of a revolute joint
    • the displacement in the case of a prismatic joint

  • A robot manipulator with nn joints will have n+1n+1 links, since each joint connects two links.

  • Number the joints from 11 to nn and the links from 00 to nn, starting from the base.

  • Joint ii, which is fixed w.r.t. link i−1i-1, connects link i−1i-1 to link ii.

  • When joint ii is actuated, link ii moves (hence, link 00 is fixed).

Denavit-Hartenberg convention

  • It is possible to carry out forward kinematic analysis even without respecting the Denavit-Hartenberg or DH convention.

  • However, the kinematic analysis of an nn-link manipulator can be extremely complex and the conventions introduced below simplify the analysis considerably.

In the DH convention, each homogeneous transformation 𝐀i\bm{A}_i is represented as a product of four basic transformations where the four quantities θi\theta_i, aia_i, did_i and αi\alpha_i are parameters associated with link ii and joint ii.

aia_i: link length
αi\alpha_i: link twist
did_i: link offset
θi\theta_i: joint angle

𝐀i=ℛz,θi𝒯z,di𝒯x,aiℛx,αi=(cθi−sθicαisθisαiaicθisθicθicαi−cθisαiaisθi0sαicαidi0001) \begin{aligned} \bm{A}_i &= \mathcal{R}_{z,\theta_i}\mathcal{T}_{z,d_i}\mathcal{T}_{x,a_i}\mathcal{R}_{x,\alpha_i} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} c_{\theta_i} & -s_{\theta_i}c_{\alpha_i} & s_{\theta_i}s_{\alpha_i} & a_ic_{\theta_i} \\ s_{\theta_i} & c_{\theta_i}c_{\alpha_i} & -c_{\theta_i}s_{\alpha_i} & a_is_{\theta_i} \\ 0 & s_{\alpha_i} & c_{\alpha_i} & d_i \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \end{aligned}

  • Recall: the matrix 𝐀i\bm{A}_i is a function of a single variable.
    • Three of the above four quantities are constant for a given link,
    • The fourth parameter, θi\theta_i for a revolute joint and did_i for a prismatic joint, is the joint variable.

Number of parameters

  • When we discussed rigid-body motion, we learned that an arbitrary homogeneous transformation matrix can be characterized by 66 numbers: 33 for rotation, 33 for translation.

  • In the DH representation, there are only four parameters: how is this possible?

    • Answer: While frame ii is required to be rigidly attached to link ii, we have considerable freedom in choosing the origin and the coordinate axes of the frame.
    • By a clever choice of the origin and the coordinate axes, it is possible to cut down the number of parameters needed from 66 to 44.

Denavit-Hartenberg Conditions

(DH1) The axis 𝐱1\bm{x}_1 is perpendicular to the axis 𝐳0\bm{z}_0:
(DH2) The axis 𝐱1\bm{x}_1 intersects the axis 𝐳0\bm{z}_0:

𝐱1⊥𝐳0\bm{x}_1 \perp \bm{z}_0.
𝐱1⋔𝐳0\bm{x}_1 \pitchfork \bm{z}_0.

  • aa: distance from axis 𝐳0\bm{z}_0 to 𝐳1\bm{z}_1, measured along 𝐱1\bm{x}_1.
  • α\alpha: angle from 𝐳0\bm{z}_0 to 𝐳1\bm{z}_1, measured along 𝐱1\bm{x}_1.
  • dd: distance from the origin o0o_0 to the intersection of 𝐱1\bm{x}_1 with 𝐳0\bm{z}_0, measured along the 𝐳0\bm{z}_0.
  • θ\theta: angle from 𝐱0\bm{x}_0 to 𝐱1\bm{x}_1, mesaured along 𝐳0\bm{z}_0.

Assigning the coordinate frames

  • One can always choose the frames Σ0,…,Σn\Sigma_0, \ldots, \Sigma_n in such a way that (DH1) and (DH2) are satisfied.
  • Sometimes, this will require placing the origin oio_i of Σi\Sigma_i in a location that may not be intuitively satisfying.
  • The choices of the coordinate frames are not unique!
    • However, the end result, i.e. the matrix 0𝐓n{^0}\bm{T}_n, will be the same! (assuming the choice of Σ0\Sigma_0 and Σn\Sigma_n coincide for the two different coordinate frame placements.)
  • The choice of 𝐳i\bm{z}_i is arbitrary: First, we assign the axes 𝐳0,…,𝐳n−1\bm{z}_0, \ldots, \bm{z}_{n-1} in an intuitively pleasing fashion: assign 𝐳i\bm{z}_i to be the axis of actuation for joint i+1i+1.
    • if joint i+1i+1 is revolute, 𝐳i\bm{z}_i is the axis of revolution of joint i+1i+1;
    • if joint i+1i+1 is prismatic, 𝐳i\bm{z}_i is the axis of translation of joint i+1i+1.
  • Second we establish the base frame. This is nearly arbitrary.
    • Choose o0o_0 to be any point on 𝐳0\bm{z}_0.
    • Choose 𝐱0\bm{x}_0, 𝐲0\bm{y}_0 in any convenient manner so long as Σ0\Sigma_0 is right-handed.
  • Third, we begin an iterative process in which we define Σi\Sigma_i using frame Σi−1\Sigma_{i-1} beginning with Σi\Sigma_i. Three cases to consider: (i) 𝐳i−1\bm{z}_{i-1} and 𝐳i\bm{z}_i are not coplanar, (ii) 𝐳i−1∥𝐳i\bm{z}_{i-1} \parallel \bm{z}_i, (iii) 𝐳i−1⋔𝐳i\bm{z}_{i-1} \pitchfork \bm{z}_i.

Assigning the coordinate frames

  • This line segment defines 𝐱i\bm{x}_i and the point where it intersects 𝐳i\bm{z}_i is the origin oio_i.
  • Choose an axis 𝐲i\bm{y}_i to form a right-handed frame.

  • To complete the construction, it is necessary to specify Σn\Sigma_n.

Summary of the DH procedure

Step 1.   Locate and label the joint axes: 𝐳0,𝐳1,…,𝐳n−1\bm{z}_0, \bm{z}_1, \ldots, \bm{z}_{n-1}.

Step 2.   Establish the base frame: set the origin anywhere along the 𝐳0\bm{z}_0 axis. Choose 𝐱0\bm{x}_0 and 𝐲0\bm{y}_0 conveniently to form a right-handed frame.

    For i=1,…,n−1i = 1, \ldots, n-1, perform steps 33 to 55.

Step 3.   Locate the origin oio_i where the common normal to 𝐳i\bm{z}_i and 𝐳i−1\bm{z}_{i-1} intersect 𝐳i\bm{z}_i. If 𝐳i⋔𝐳i−1\bm{z}_i \pitchfork \bm{z}_{i-1}, locate oio_i at this intersection. If 𝐳i∥𝐳i−1\bm{z}_i \parallel \bm{z}_{i-1}, locate oio_i at any convenient position along 𝐳i\bm{z}_i.

Step 5.   Establish the 𝐲i\bm{y}_i to complete a right-handed frame.

If the nthn^{\text{th}} joint is revolute, set 𝐳n=𝐚\bm{z}_n = \bm{a} parallel to 𝐳n−1\bm{z}_{n-1}. Summary of the DH procedure

Step 7.   Create a table of DH parameters aia_i, did_i, αi\alpha_i, θi\theta_i:

  • did_i: distance along 𝐳i−1\bm{z}_{i-1} from oi−1o_{i-1} to the intersection of the 𝐱i\bm{x}_i and 𝐳i−1\bm{z}_{i-1} axes. If joints ii is prismatic, did_i is a variable.
  • αi\alpha_i: the angle from 𝐳i−1\bm{z}_{i-1} to 𝐳i\bm{z}_i measured about 𝐱i\bm{x}_i.
  • θi\theta_i: the angle from 𝐱i−1\bm{x}_{i-1} to 𝐱i\bm{x}_i measured about 𝐳i−1\bm{z}_{i-1}. If joint ii is revolute, θi\theta_i is a variable.

Derivation of the Jacobian

  • Consider an nn-link manipulator with joint variables q1,…,qnq_1, \ldots, q_n and let 0𝐓n{^0}\bm{T}_n denote the transformation from the end-effector frame Σn\Sigma_n to the base frame Σ0\Sigma_0.

0𝐓n=(0𝐑n(𝐪)0𝐨n(𝐪)𝟎1) {^0}\bm{T}_n = \begin{pmatrix} {^0}\bm{R}_n(\bm{q}) & {^0}\bm{o}_n(\bm{q}) \\ \bm{0} & 1 \end{pmatrix}

  • The objective is to relate the linear and angular velocity (the twist) of the end-effector Σn\Sigma_n to the vector of joint rates or velocities 𝐪̇(t)\dot{\bm{q}}(t).


  • With 𝐉v,𝐉ω∈ℝ3×n\bm{J}_v, \bm{J}_\omega \in \mathbb{R}^{3\times n}, we seek expressions of the form


  • With 𝐉\bm{J} denoting the manipulator Jacobian or Jacobian, together they can be written as 𝛈=𝐉𝐪̇\bm{\eta} = \bm{J}\dot{\bm{q}}.

0𝐯n=𝐉v𝐪̇0𝛚n=𝐉ω𝐪̇ \begin{aligned} {^0}\bm{v}_n &= \bm{J}_v \dot{\bm{q}} \\ {^0}\bm{\omega}_n &= \bm{J}_\omega \dot{\bm{q}} \end{aligned}

𝛈=(0𝐯n0𝛚n)and𝐉=(𝐉v𝐉ω)6×n \bm{\eta} = \begin{pmatrix} {^0}\bm{v}_n \\ {^0}\bm{\omega}_n \end{pmatrix} \quad \text{and} \quad \bm{J} = \begin{pmatrix} \bm{J}_v \\ \bm{J}_\omega \end{pmatrix}_{6 \times n}

  • This twist 𝛈\bm{\eta} is NOT the derivative of a pose variable, since the angular velocity vector is not the derivative of any particular time-varying quantity.

0𝐨̇n=∑i=1n∂0𝐨n∂qiq̇i.{^0}\dot{\bm{o}}_n = \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\partial {^0}\bm{o}_n}{\partial q_i}\dot{q}_i.

Inverse Kinematics

The general inverse kinematics problem

  • Given a 4×44 \times 4 homogeneous transformation 𝐇=(𝐑𝐝𝟎1)∈𝐒𝐄(3)\bm{H} = \begin{pmatrix} \bm{R} & \bm{d} \\ \bm{0} & 1 \end{pmatrix} \in \bm{SE}(3), find a solution, or possibly multiple solutions of the equation 0𝐓n(q1,…,qn)=𝐀1(q1)⋯𝐀(qn)=𝐇. {^0}\bm{T}_n(q_1, \ldots, q_n) = \bm{A}_1(q_1) \cdots \bm{A}(q_n) = \bm{H}.

  • This matrix equation has sixteen scalar equations to be solved for nn variables q1,…,qnq_1, \ldots, q_n.

  • Four of these equations are trivial (last row of 𝐇\bm{H}) so the remaining twelve equations are 𝐭ij(q1,…,qn)=𝐡ij, for i=1,2,3,j=1,2,3,4.\bm{t}_{ij}(q_1, \ldots, q_n) = \bm{h}_{ij}, \text{ for } \quad i = 1, 2, 3, \; \; j = 1, 2, 3, 4.

  • Typically these equations are much too difficult to solve directly in closed-form.

    • kinematic structure of the manipulator must be exploited to get a closed-form solution.

  • Unlike the forward kinematics problem, the inverse kinematics problem may or may not have a solution.

    • Even if a solution exists, it may or may not be unique.
    • Since the forward kinematic equations are in general complicated nonlinear functions of the joint variables, the solutions may be difficult to obtain even when they do exist.

  • Once a solution is identified, it must be further checked to see whether or not it satisfies all constraints on ther anges of possible joint motions.

Numerical inverse kinematics

  • Let’s consider an iterative, numerical algorithms for the computation of inverse kinematics.
    • They are increasingly popular due to the availability of high-performance computation and the advent of open-source software.
    • When closed-form solutions do not exist or if the manipulator is redundant, a recourse to numerical methods may be the better option.
  • Let 𝐱d∈ℝm\bm{x}^d \in \mathbb{R}^m be a vector of Cartesian coordinates: 𝐱d\bm{x}^d could represent
    • the wrist center point (m=3m=3),

Jacobian inverse method

  • We expand the forward kinematics map 𝐟(𝐪)\bm{f}(\bm{q}) in a Taylor series about a configuration 𝐪d\bm{q}^d, such that 𝐱d=𝐟(𝐪d)\bm{x}^d = \bm{f}(\bm{q}^d) to obtain 𝐟(𝐪)=𝐟(𝐪d)+𝐉(𝐪d)(𝐪−𝐪d)+h.o.t., \bm{f}(\bm{q}) = \bm{f}(\bm{q}^d) + \bm{J}(\bm{q}^d)(\bm{q} - \bm{q}^d) + \text{h.o.t.}, where we take 𝐉=𝐉a(𝐪)\bm{J} = \bm{J}_a(\bm{q}) as the analytic Jacobian.

  • Neglecting h.o.t., we have 𝐪d−𝐪=𝐉−1(𝐪)(𝐱d−𝐟(𝐪))\bm{q}^d - \bm{q} = \bm{J}^{-1}(\bm{q}) \left(\bm{x}^d - \bm{f}(\bm{q}) \right) assuming that the Jacobian is invertible.

  • To find a solution for 𝐪d\bm{q}^d, we begin with an initial guess, 𝐪0\bm{q}_0 and form a sequence of successive estimates, 𝐪0,𝐪1,𝐪2,…\bm{q}_0, \bm{q}_1, \bm{q}_2, \ldots, as 𝐪k=𝐪k−1+αk𝐉−1(𝐱d−𝐟(𝐪k−1)),k=1,2,….\bm{q}_k = \bm{q}_{k-1} + \alpha_k \bm{J}^{-1} \left(\bm{x}^d - \bm{f}(\bm{q}_{k-1}) \right), \;\; k= 1,2,\ldots.

    • The step size αk>0\alpha_k > 0 can be adjusted over each iteration to aid convergence.
    • If the Jacobian is not invertible, then one may use the pseudoinverse 𝐉†\bm{J}^\dagger.
    • For m≤nm \leq n, we define the right pseudoinverse as 𝐉†≜𝐉⊤(𝐉𝐉⊤)−1\bm{J}^\dagger \triangleq \bm{J}^\top \left(\bm{J}\bm{J}^\top\right)^{-1}

This equation is based on a first-order approximation of the inverse kinematics so only local convergence can be expected.

Jacobian transpose method

  • We define an optimization problem:   min𝐪𝐅(𝐪)=min𝐪12(𝐟(𝐪)−𝐱d)⊤(𝐟(𝐪)−𝐱d)\min_\bm{q} \bm{F}(\bm{q}) = \min_\bm{q} \frac{1}{2}\left( \bm{f}(\bm{q}) - \bm{x}^d \right)^\top \left( \bm{f}(\bm{q}) - \bm{x}^d \right).
  • The gradient of the above cost function 𝐅(𝐪)\bm{F}(\bm{q}) is given by:   ∇𝐅(𝐪)=𝐉⊤(𝐪)(𝐟(𝐪)−𝐱d).\nabla \bm{F}(\bm{q}) = \bm{J}^\top(\bm{q}) \left( \bm{f}(\bm{q}) - \bm{x}^d \right).
  • A gradient descent algorithm to minimize 𝐅(𝐪)\bm{F}(\bm{q}) is then 𝐪k=𝐪k−1−αk∇𝐅(𝐪)=𝐪k−1+αk𝐉⊤(𝐱d−𝐟(𝐪k−1)),k=1,2,….\bm{q}_k = \bm{q}_{k-1} - \alpha_k \; \nabla\bm{F}(\bm{q}) = \bm{q}_{k-1} + \alpha_k \bm{J}^{\top} \left(\bm{x}^d - \bm{f}(\bm{q}_{k-1}) \right), \;\; k= 1,2,\ldots.

Comparison of inverse and transpose methods

  • Jacobian transpose is easier to compute than the Jacobian inverse.
  • Jacobian transpose does not suffer from configuration singularities.
  • The converge of the Jacobian transpose method, in terms of number of iterations, may be slower than the Jacobian inverse method.

Inverse position: a geometric approach

  • Most six-DoF manipulator designs are kinematically simple rendering the geometric approach simple and effective.
    • It is partly due to the difficulty of the general inverse kinematics problem that manipulator designs have evolved to their present state.
  • The complexity of the inverse kinematics problem increases with the number of nonzero DH parameters.
    • For most manipulators, many of the aia_i, did_i are zero, the αi\alpha_i are 00 or ±π2\pm \frac{\pi}{2}, etc.
    • In these cases especially, a geometric approach is simplest and most natural.
  • The general idea of the geometric approach is to solve for the joint variable qiq_i by projecting the manipulator onto the 𝐱i−1−𝐲i−1\bm{x}_{i-1}-\bm{y}_{i-1} plane and solving a simple trigonometry problem.
    • For example, to solve for θ1\theta_1, we project the arm onto the 𝐱0\bm{x}_0-𝐲0\bm{y}_0 plane and use trigonometry to find θ1\theta_1.
    • We will illustrate this method with two important examples: the spherical (RRP) and the articulated (RRR) arms.
